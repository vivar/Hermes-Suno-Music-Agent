# Base Music Genres Reference (Comprehensive Edition)

This is an extensive reference covering the major foundational music genres with deep production, arrangement, sonic, and creative detail. These base genres form the building blocks for nearly all modern and hybrid music styles.

The goal of this reference is to give both human producers and AI-assisted workflows the knowledge needed to create authentic, high-quality music within each genre — and to intelligently blend genres when desired.

## Pop

**Core Identity**
Pop is the most commercially dominant genre. It prioritizes catchiness, emotional clarity, broad appeal, and polished production. The goal is to create songs that are instantly memorable and work across many demographics.

**Major Sub-Genres**
- Mainstream / Radio Pop
- Dance-Pop
- Electropop
- Teen Pop
- Indie Pop
- Hyperpop

**Instrumentation**
- **Drums**: Tight, punchy, often programmed or heavily sampled. Modern pop uses layered kicks, crisp snares/claps, and bright hi-hats.
- **Bass**: Usually synth bass or sub-bass. Needs to be clean and prominent but not overpowering.
- **Harmonic Foundation**: Synth pads, electric piano, or clean electric guitars.
- **Melodic Elements**: Synth leads, plucks, bells, or processed guitars.
- **Vocals**: Lead vocal is almost always the focal point. Heavy use of doubling, harmonies, and ad-libs.

**Rhythmic & Harmonic Characteristics**
- Straight, danceable rhythms (usually 4/4 at 90–130 BPM)
- Simple but effective chord progressions (I–V–vi–IV is extremely common)
- Emphasis on the “hook” — the most memorable melodic or lyrical phrase

**Vocal Approach**
- Clear, confident, and emotionally direct
- Modern pop vocals are heavily processed (Auto-Tune, compression, saturation, reverb, delay)
- Harmonies and stacked vocals are essential for thickness

**Common Arrangement Structures**
- Intro → Verse 1 → Pre-Chorus → Chorus → Verse 2 → Pre-Chorus → Chorus → Bridge → Final Chorus → Outro
- Many modern pop songs use shortened versions (2:30–3:00)

**Mixing Priorities**
- Vocal must sit clearly on top of everything
- Low end should be tight and controlled (not boomy)
- High end should be bright and exciting but not harsh
- Stereo width is used strategically — often wider in choruses

**Production Tips**
- Focus on hook strength above all else
- Use reference tracks from current chart hits
- Modern pop benefits from “in-the-box” polished processing
- Automation of energy and effects is crucial

**AI / Suno Prompting Guidance**
When prompting for pop, emphasize:
- “Catchy pop hook”, “radio-ready”, “polished production”, “modern pop vocal”
- Specify vocal style: “bright female vocal”, “smooth male vocal”, “layered harmonies”
- Mention current production trends when relevant

**Classic Reference Tracks**
- “Blinding Lights” – The Weeknd
- “Levitating” – Dua Lipa
- “As It Was” – Harry Styles

## Rock

**Core Identity**
Rock is built around electric guitars, live band energy, and emotional authenticity. It values groove, attitude, and instrumental interplay.

**Major Sub-Genres**
- Classic Rock
- Hard Rock
- Alternative Rock
- Indie Rock
- Punk Rock
- Garage Rock

**Instrumentation**
- **Drums**: Acoustic drum kit with strong kick and snare. Room mics are often used for natural space.
- **Bass**: Electric bass guitar — usually follows the root notes but can be melodic.
- **Guitars**: Rhythm guitar (distorted or clean) + lead guitar. Power chords are foundational.
- **Vocals**: Can range from clean and melodic to raspy and aggressive.

**Rhythmic & Harmonic Characteristics**
- Strong backbeat (snare on 2 and 4)
- Power chords and open chords
- Blues-based or modal harmony in many sub-genres

**Vocal Approach**
- Expressive and often gritty
- Can be shouted, sung, or half-sung depending on sub-genre

**Common Arrangement Structures**
- Intro → Verse → Chorus → Verse → Chorus → Bridge/Solo → Chorus → Outro
- Guitar solos are a signature element in many rock songs

**Mixing Priorities**
- Guitars need clarity and separation (especially when multiple guitars are used)
- Drums should feel powerful but not overpower the guitars
- Vocals must cut through the wall of sound

**Production Tips**
- Capture good live takes when possible
- Use analog-style saturation and tape emulation
- Layering guitars (different tones and positions) creates thickness

**AI / Suno Prompting Guidance**
Emphasize:
- “Live band energy”, “driving guitars”, “rock vocal with attitude”
- Specify sub-genre when possible (e.g., “90s alternative rock”, “classic 70s rock”)

**Classic Reference Tracks**
- “Hotel California” – Eagles
- “Back in Black” – AC/DC
- “Smells Like Teen Spirit” – Nirvana

## Hip-Hop / Rap

**Core Identity**
Hip-hop is rhythm and lyric-driven. The beat is the foundation, and the vocal (usually rapping) sits on top with rhythmic precision and attitude.

**Major Sub-Genres**
- Trap
- Boom Bap
- Drill
- Conscious Rap
- Melodic Rap
- Lo-fi Hip-Hop

**Instrumentation**
- **Drums**: 808s, crisp snares, rolling hi-hats, and heavy kicks. Trap often uses rolling triplet hi-hats.
- **Bass**: 808 bass is signature — deep, sustained, and often pitch-bent.
- **Melodic Elements**: Sampled loops, synth melodies, or atmospheric pads.
- **Vocals**: Rapping with rhythmic delivery. Modern hip-hop often blends singing and rapping.

**Rhythmic & Harmonic Characteristics**
- Strong emphasis on the “pocket” and groove
- Often uses minor keys or dark modes
- Repetition with subtle variation is common

**Vocal Approach**
- Rhythmic and percussive
- Modern rap often uses Auto-Tune or melodic processing
- Ad-libs and background vocals add energy

**Common Arrangement Structures**
- Intro → Hook → Verse → Hook → Verse → Hook → Outro
- Many modern tracks are shorter (2:00–3:00)

**Mixing Priorities**
- 808 must be controlled but powerful
- Vocals need to be clear and forward
- Hi-hats and percussion should have space and clarity

**Production Tips**
- Sidechain melodic elements to the 808/kick
- Use saturation and distortion on 808s for presence
- Sample selection and chopping is an art form

**AI / Suno Prompting Guidance**
Use terms like:
- “trap beat with heavy 808s”, “boom bap drums”, “melodic rap flow”
- Specify vocal delivery: “aggressive rap”, “melodic singing rap”, “storytelling rap”

**Classic Reference Tracks**
- “Sicko Mode” – Travis Scott
- “The Box” – Roddy Ricch
- “Lose Yourself” – Eminem

## Electronic / EDM

**Core Identity**
Electronic music is built around synthesizers, programmed drums, and sound design. Energy management and sonic impact are paramount.

**Major Sub-Genres**
- House
- Techno
- Trance
- Dubstep
- Future Bass
- Synthwave
- Drum & Bass

**Instrumentation**
- **Drums**: Programmed kicks, claps, hi-hats, and percussion. Often uses sidechained compression.
- **Bass**: Synth bass, Reese bass, wobble bass, or sub-bass.
- **Synths**: Leads, pads, plucks, supersaws, and evolving textures.
- **Sound Design**: Risers, impacts, sweeps, and atmospheric effects.

**Rhythmic & Harmonic Characteristics**
- Four-on-the-floor in many sub-genres
- Build-ups and massive drops
- Often uses minor keys and modal harmony

**Vocal Approach**
- Can be featured or completely absent
- When present, often heavily processed or chopped

**Common Arrangement Structures**
- Intro → Build-up → Drop → Breakdown → Build-up → Drop → Outro
- Tension and release are central to the genre

**Mixing Priorities**
- Wide stereo image
- Powerful, controlled low end
- Clarity in the midrange despite dense arrangements

**Production Tips**
- Automation is everything — volume, filter cutoff, effects
- Sound design and synthesis skills are highly valuable
- Reference tracks from the exact sub-genre are essential

**AI / Suno Prompting Guidance**
Specify:
- “future bass with supersaw leads”, “dark techno”, “uplifting trance”
- Mention specific techniques: “sidechained pads”, “wobble bass”, “build-up with riser”

**Classic Reference Tracks**
- “Strobe” – deadmau5
- “Animals” – Martin Garrix
- “Scary Monsters and Nice Sprites” – Skrillex

## Country

**Core Identity**
Country music emphasizes storytelling, emotional honesty, and a blend of traditional and modern sounds. It values authenticity and relatability.

**Major Sub-Genres**
- Traditional Country
- Modern Country
- Country Rock
- Country
- Bluegrass

**Instrumentation**
- **Acoustic Guitar**: Rhythm foundation (often fingerpicked or strummed)
- **Electric Guitar**: Often with twang or slide
- **Pedal Steel Guitar**: Signature weeping, sliding sound
- **Fiddle / Violin**: Common in traditional and bluegrass styles
- **Banjo / Mandolin**: Especially in bluegrass and traditional country
- **Bass & Drums**: Provide the groove

**Rhythmic & Harmonic Characteristics**
- Straight or swung rhythms depending on sub-genre
- Simple, effective chord progressions
- Strong emphasis on the narrative

**Vocal Approach**
- Clear, often with a slight twang or drawl
- Storytelling delivery is more important than technical perfection
- Harmonies are common (especially in traditional country)

**Common Arrangement Structures**
- Intro → Verse → Chorus → Verse → Chorus → Bridge → Chorus → Outro
- Instrumental breaks (especially fiddle or guitar solos) are common

**Mixing Priorities**
- Vocal must be clear and forward
- Acoustic instruments should feel natural and warm
- Low end should be present but not dominant

**Production Tips**
- Modern country often blends pop production techniques
- Preserve the organic, human feel even when using heavy processing
- Pedal steel and fiddle are powerful emotional tools

**AI / Suno Prompting Guidance**
Emphasize:
- “storytelling country vocal”, “modern country production”, “pedal steel guitar”
- Specify era or sub-style when relevant

**Classic Reference Tracks**
- “Tennessee Whiskey” – Chris Stapleton
- “The House That Built Me” – Miranda Lambert
- “Wagon Wheel” – Darius Rucker

## R&B / Soul

**Core Identity**
R&B and Soul prioritize emotional vocal performance, groove, and rich harmonic textures. It sits between pop, hip-hop, and jazz.

**Major Sub-Genres**
- Classic Soul
- Contemporary R&B
- Neo-Soul
- Alternative R&B

**Instrumentation**
- **Drums**: Tight, groovy, often with a strong backbeat
- **Bass**: Melodic and prominent
- **Keys**: Electric piano, organ, or synth pads
- **Guitars**: Clean or lightly processed
- **Horns and Strings**: Used for richness and punctuation

**Rhythmic & Harmonic Characteristics**
- Strong pocket and swing feel
- Rich chord extensions (7ths, 9ths, 11ths)
- Often uses minor keys with soulful movement

**Vocal Approach**
- Highly expressive and emotional
- Heavy use of runs, ad-libs, and vocal layering
- Modern R&B often blends singing and rapping

**Common Arrangement Structures**
- Intro → Verse → Pre-Chorus → Chorus → Verse → Chorus → Bridge → Final Chorus

**Mixing Priorities**
- Warm, smooth vocal tone
- Tight but musical low end
- Rich midrange presence

**Production Tips**
- Vocal production is central — compression, saturation, and reverb are key
- Groove and feel matter more than complexity
- Background vocals and harmonies add significant depth

**AI / Suno Prompting Guidance**
Use terms like:
- “soulful R&B vocal”, “neo-soul groove”, “smooth contemporary R&B”
- Mention vocal techniques: “vocal runs”, “layered harmonies”, “emotional delivery”

**Classic Reference Tracks**
- “Superstition” – Stevie Wonder
- “Blinding Lights” – The Weeknd (modern R&B influence)
- “Get You” – Daniel Caesar

## Folk / Acoustic

**Core Identity**
Folk music emphasizes intimacy, storytelling, and organic instrumentation. It values authenticity and emotional connection over polished production.

**Major Sub-Genres**
- Traditional Folk
- Contemporary Folk
- Indie Folk
- Americana

**Instrumentation**
- **Acoustic Guitar**: The foundation (fingerstyle or strumming)
- **Vocals**: Intimate and honest
- **Additional Instruments**: Harmonica, banjo, violin, mandolin, upright bass, piano

**Rhythmic & Harmonic Characteristics**
- Simple, effective progressions
- Often fingerpicked patterns or gentle strumming
- Emphasis on space and dynamics

**Vocal Approach**
- Natural and conversational
- Storytelling delivery
- Minimal processing

**Common Arrangement Structures**
- Simple verse-chorus or verse-verse-chorus forms
- Instrumental breaks are common

**Mixing Priorities**
- Natural acoustic tone
- Vocal intimacy and clarity
- Subtle, tasteful use of reverb

**Production Tips**
- Less is more — avoid over-processing
- Capture good live performances when possible
- Focus on songwriting and emotional delivery

**AI / Suno Prompting Guidance**
Emphasize:
- “intimate acoustic folk”, “storytelling vocal”, “organic production”
- Mention specific instruments when desired

**Classic Reference Tracks**
- “Hallelujah” – Leonard Cohen / Jeff Buckley
- “Skinny Love” – Bon Iver
- “Fast Car” – Tracy Chapman

## Metal

**Core Identity**
Metal is defined by heavy, distorted guitars, aggressive rhythms, and high energy. It values technical skill, intensity, and atmosphere.

**Major Sub-Genres**
- Heavy Metal
- Thrash Metal
- Death Metal
- Black Metal
- Doom Metal
- Metalcore

**Instrumentation**
- **Guitars**: Heavily distorted, often downtuned. Uses palm muting and fast alternate picking.
- **Bass**: Often distorted or layered with the guitars
- **Drums**: Double-kick drumming, blast beats, and powerful toms
- **Vocals**: Can be harsh (screams, growls) or clean

**Rhythmic & Harmonic Characteristics**
- Fast, precise rhythms
- Minor keys and diminished chords
- Complex time signatures in some sub-genres

**Vocal Approach**
- Ranges from melodic singing to extreme harsh vocals
- Many modern metal bands use both clean and harsh vocals

**Common Arrangement Structures**
- Verse-Chorus with breakdowns, solos, and bridges
- High energy throughout with dynamic contrast

**Mixing Priorities**
- Guitar tightness and clarity
- Powerful, controlled drum impact
- Low-end management (especially with downtuned guitars)

**Production Tips**
- Tight rhythm section is essential
- Modern metal uses heavy sample replacement and processing
- Guitar tone is one of the most important elements

**AI / Suno Prompting Guidance**
Specify:
- “heavy distorted guitars”, “double-kick drums”, “aggressive metal vocal”
- Mention sub-genre when relevant (e.g., “doom metal atmosphere”, “thrash metal energy”)

**Classic Reference Tracks**
- “Master of Puppets” – Metallica
- “Paranoid” – Black Sabbath
- “The Pretender” – Foo Fighters (modern rock/metal influence)

## Jazz

**Core Identity**
Jazz is defined by improvisation, complex harmony, and rhythmic sophistication. It values interaction between musicians and expressive freedom.

**Major Sub-Genres**
- Bebop
- Hard Bop
- Cool Jazz
- Modal Jazz
- Fusion
- Smooth Jazz

**Instrumentation**
- **Piano**: Chordal and melodic foundation
- **Bass**: Upright bass (walking lines) or electric
- **Drums**: Often uses brushes; focuses on swing and dynamics
- **Horns**: Saxophone, trumpet, trombone
- **Guitar**: Common in some styles

**Rhythmic & Harmonic Characteristics**
- Swing feel (or straight in some modern styles)
- Rich chord extensions and substitutions
- Modal harmony and improvisation

**Vocal Approach**
- Scatting, melodic improvisation, and emotional delivery
- Can be instrumental-focused with no vocals

**Common Arrangement Structures**
- Head → Solos → Head
- Many modern jazz pieces are through-composed or open-ended

**Mixing Priorities**
- Natural instrument tones
- Good separation between instruments
- Warm, analog-style sound

**Production Tips**
- Capture live interaction when possible
- Focus on dynamics and musical conversation
- Avoid over-compression

**AI / Suno Prompting Guidance**
Use terms like:
- “bebop jazz”, “modal jazz improvisation”, “smooth jazz groove”
- Mention specific instruments and swing feel

**Classic Reference Tracks**
- “So What” – Miles Davis
- “Take Five” – Dave Brubeck
- “Autumn Leaves” – various artists

## Reggae / Dub

**Core Identity**
Reggae and Dub are defined by off-beat rhythms, heavy bass, and spacious production. The “one drop” rhythm and heavy use of effects are signature elements.

**Major Sub-Genres**
- Roots Reggae
- Dub
- Dancehall
- Ska
- Rocksteady

**Instrumentation**
- **Drums**: One-drop rhythm (bass drum on 3, snare on 3, no bass drum on 1)
- **Bass**: Deep, melodic, and prominent
- **Guitar**: Skanking on the off-beats (2 and 4)
- **Organ / Keys**: Often with heavy reverb and delay
- **Horns**: Used for stabs and melodies

**Rhythmic & Harmonic Characteristics**
- Strong off-beat emphasis
- Simple, repetitive chord progressions
- Heavy use of space and echo

**Vocal Approach**
- Melodic toasting or singing
- Often features call-and-response

**Common Arrangement Structures**
- Repetitive grooves with space for improvisation and effects
- Dub versions often strip elements and add heavy echo/delay

**Mixing Priorities**
- Massive, controlled low end
- Clear space in the mids
- Heavy, creative use of delay and reverb

**Production Tips**
- The “one drop” rhythm is foundational
- Effects processing (especially delay and reverb) is a major part of the sound
- Space and dynamics are more important than density

**AI / Suno Prompting Guidance**
Emphasize:
- “one drop reggae rhythm”, “heavy dub bass”, “off-beat skanking guitar”
- Mention effects: “heavy echo and delay”

**Classic Reference Tracks**
- “No Woman, No Cry” – Bob Marley
- “Police and Thieves” – Junior Murvin
- Various Lee “Scratch” Perry dub productions

## Blues

**Core Identity**
Blues is the foundation of much of modern music. It emphasizes emotional expression, the 12-bar form, and a gritty, soulful sound.

**Major Sub-Genres**
- Delta Blues
- Chicago Blues
- Electric Blues
- Blues Rock

**Instrumentation**
- **Guitar**: Electric or acoustic. Signature bends, slides, and vibrato.
- **Bass**: Walking bass lines or steady root notes
- **Drums**: Simple, driving patterns
- **Piano / Harmonica**: Common additional instruments

**Rhythmic & Harmonic Characteristics**
- 12-bar blues form (most common)
- Blues scale and blue notes
- Straight or swung rhythms

**Vocal Approach**
- Expressive, gritty, and emotional
- Call-and-response with instruments is common

**Common Arrangement Structures**
- 12-bar blues repeated with variations
- Room for guitar solos and vocal improvisation

**Mixing Priorities**
- Guitar and vocal tone are central
- Warm, natural low end
- Preserve dynamics and feel

**Production Tips**
- Feel and expression matter more than technical perfection
- Analog-style saturation and warmth work extremely well
- Leave space for the music to breathe

**AI / Suno Prompting Guidance**
Use terms like:
- “Chicago blues”, “delta blues slide guitar”, “blues rock energy”
- Mention 12-bar form and blues scale when relevant

**Classic Reference Tracks**
- “Cross Road Blues” – Robert Johnson
- “Hoochie Coochie Man” – Muddy Waters
- “The Thrill Is Gone” – B.B. King

## K-Pop

**Core Identity**
K-Pop is a highly polished, genre-blending style that combines elements of pop, hip-hop, EDM, R&B, and rock. It is known for its precision, visual presentation, catchy hooks, and sophisticated production. K-Pop prioritizes both musical quality and performance.

**Major Sub-Genres & Styles**
- Mainstream / Idol K-Pop
- K-Pop Ballads
- Hip-Hop / Rap-focused K-Pop
- EDM / Dance K-Pop
- R&B / Soul K-Pop
- Rock / Band K-Pop
- Hyperpop / Experimental K-Pop

**Instrumentation**
- **Drums**: Tight, punchy, and often heavily processed. Modern K-Pop frequently uses layered kicks, crisp snares, and bright, rolling hi-hats. Trap-influenced beats are very common.
- **Bass**: Usually synth bass or 808-style sub-bass. Needs to be clean, powerful, and well-controlled.
- **Synths & Production Elements**: Supersaw leads, plucks, pads, risers, impacts, and atmospheric textures. Sound design is extremely important.
- **Guitars**: Clean electric guitars or distorted guitars (especially in rock-influenced tracks).
- **Vocals**: Multiple vocalists with distinct roles (main vocal, lead vocal, rap, sub-vocal). Heavy use of layering, harmonies, and ad-libs.

**Rhythmic & Harmonic Characteristics**
- Usually 4/4 time with tempos ranging from 80–150 BPM
- Mix of straight rhythms and trap-influenced triplet feels
- Chord progressions often blend Western pop with subtle jazz or R&B influences
- Strong emphasis on the “hook” and repetitive, memorable phrases

**Vocal Approach**
- Highly trained and precise vocals
- Multiple members often share lines (group vocals)
- Frequent use of rap sections alongside melodic singing
- Heavy processing: Auto-Tune, compression, reverb, delay, and vocal layering
- Emotional delivery in ballads; energetic and confident in dance tracks

**Common Arrangement Structures**
- Intro → Verse 1 (Member 1) → Pre-Chorus → Chorus → Rap → Verse 2 → Pre-Chorus → Chorus → Bridge → Final Chorus / Dance Break → Outro
- Many songs feature a “dance break” or high-energy section near the end
- Songs are often 2:30–3:30 in length

**Mixing Priorities**
- Vocals must be extremely clear and forward
- Low end should be tight and powerful (especially 808s)
- High end should be bright and exciting
- Wide stereo image with careful management of center vs. sides
- All elements must feel polished and professional

**Production Tips**
- K-Pop production is extremely detailed and layered
- Sound design and effects automation are crucial
- Many tracks use “in-the-box” production with high-quality virtual instruments and plugins
- Reference tracks from current K-Pop hits are essential for authenticity
- The production must support both audio and visual performance

**AI / Suno Prompting Guidance**
When prompting for K-Pop, emphasize:
- “K-Pop idol style”, “catchy K-Pop hook”, “polished K-Pop production”
- Specify vocal roles: “bright female lead vocal”, “rap verse”, “group harmonies”
- Mention production style: “supersaw leads”, “trap-influenced beat”, “dance break”
- Reference current trends (e.g., “2024 K-Pop style”, “NewJeans-inspired”, “BTS-style”)

**Classic Reference Tracks**
- “Dynamite” – BTS
- “Pink Venom” – BLACKPINK
- “Supernova” – aespa
- “Attention” – NewJeans
- “Love Dive” – IVE

## Genre Blending Notes

Most modern music is hybrid. When blending genres, identify the dominant base elements and combine characteristics thoughtfully.

**Common Blends**
- Country + Rock → Country Rock
- Hip-Hop + R&B → Contemporary R&B / Melodic Rap
- Electronic + Pop → Dance-Pop / Electropop
- Rock + Metal → Hard Rock / Metalcore
- Folk + Indie → Indie Folk / Americana
- Pop + Hip-Hop + EDM → Modern K-Pop

**Best Practice**
When creating hybrid styles:
1. Choose 2–3 base genres maximum
2. Decide which genre dominates the rhythm, harmony, and production
3. Use instrumentation and production techniques from the secondary genres as flavor
4. Reference tracks from both genres to maintain authenticity

This reference can continue to grow with more sub-genre depth and specific production techniques over time.
