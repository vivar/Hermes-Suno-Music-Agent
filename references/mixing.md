# Mixing Reference for Beginners

Mixing is the process of balancing and polishing all the elements in your song so they sound clear, powerful, and professional together. Many beginners focus too much on plugins and not enough on balance and intent.

## The Golden Rule of Mixing

**Balance is more important than processing.**

A well-balanced mix with almost no plugins will usually sound better than a poorly balanced mix with expensive plugins.

## Recommended Mixing Order

### Phase 1: Preparation
1. **Gain Staging**  
   Keep peaks around -18 dBFS to -12 dBFS. This gives you headroom for processing later.

2. **Static Mix (No Plugins)**  
   Set basic volume levels and panning first. Many mixes improve dramatically from this step alone.

### Phase 2: Core Processing
3. **EQ (Corrective First)**  
   - High-pass filter low rumble on everything except bass and kick
   - Cut muddy frequencies (200–400 Hz) on most elements
   - Cut before you boost

4. **Compression**  
   - Control dynamics on vocals, drums, and bass
   - Use moderate ratios (3:1 to 6:1) for most elements
   - Focus on making things sit consistently rather than making them loud

5. **Panning & Stereo Width**  
   - Keep important elements (vocals, bass, kick, snare) in the center
   - Spread supporting elements (pads, guitars, background vocals) wider
   - Use stereo imaging tools sparingly

### Phase 3: Creative & Space
6. **Saturation / Harmonic Exciters** (optional)
7. **Reverb & Delay**  
   - Use send tracks instead of inserting on every channel
   - Different reverbs for different elements (short for drums, longer for vocals)
8. **Automation**  
   - Volume automation is extremely powerful
   - Automate filters, effects, and levels throughout the song

### Phase 4: Final Polish
9. **Mastering** (last step)
   - Light EQ, compression, and limiting
   - Aim for competitive loudness without destroying dynamics

## Essential Mixing Concepts

### EQ
- Cut more than you boost
- High-pass everything that doesn't need low end
- Make space for each element in the frequency spectrum

### Compression
- Attack: How fast the compressor reacts
- Release: How fast it lets go
- Ratio: How much it reduces the signal
- Threshold: When compression starts

### Reverb & Space
- Reverb creates depth (front to back)
- Panning creates width (left to right)
- Together they create a 3D mix

### Reference Tracks
This is the single best mixing tool for beginners:
- Import a professional track in the same genre
- Match volume levels
- Constantly A/B compare
- Ask: "What is different about the low end? The vocals? The width?"

## Common Mixing Mistakes

- Making everything too loud
- Using too many plugins
- Not using reference tracks
- Ignoring panning and stereo width
- Over-compressing everything
- Adding reverb to every track individually

## Beginner Mixing Checklist

- Are all elements audible and balanced?
- Is there a clear focal point (usually the vocal)?
- Does the mix have width and depth?
- Does it sound good on multiple systems (headphones, speakers, phone)?
- Have I compared it to professional reference tracks?

## Pro Tips

- Take breaks — your ears get tired quickly
- Mix at moderate volumes (not too loud)
- Check your mix in mono occasionally
- Trust your ears more than the meters

Mixing is a skill that improves dramatically with practice. The more tracks you finish, the better your mixing will become.
